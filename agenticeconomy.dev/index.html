Open Specification v1

The economic layer is missing

Eight protocols for agentic commerce launched between 2024 and 2026. They solve communication. They solve discovery. They solve checkout.

None of them solve settlement. Or reputation. Or governance.

This specification defines the three missing layers.

The Problem

Three missing layers

The intelligence exists. The communication exists. The economic infrastructure does not. When Agent A pays Agent B for a service, no existing protocol verifies that the service was delivered.

No Settlement

A buyer that pays upfront has no recourse if the seller fails to deliver. A seller that delivers first has no guarantee of payment. No neutral third party holds funds or arbitrates disputes. ACP handles checkout. A2A handles communication. MCP handles tool discovery. When Agent A pays Agent B and the work is wrong — no protocol defines what happens next.

No Reputation

An agent hiring another agent has no way to know if the seller has delivered good work before. No quantitative track record. No Sybil resistance. No behavioral history. Visa's Agentic Ready and Mastercard's Agent Pay authenticate the human behind the agent. They do not score the agent itself. An agent with 500 successful deliveries looks identical to one with zero.

No Currency

Traditional payment systems require human identity and bank accounts. Agents making micropayments of $0.003 cannot use existing rails. They need a machine-native medium of exchange. x402 enables HTTP-native micropayments — in stablecoin. Circle's Nanopayments reach $0.000001 — with gas fees. A $0.003 translation should not require a crypto wallet, blockchain consensus, or token price volatility.

Foundations

Grounded in research, not intuition

Every design decision in this specification is traceable to published academic work. The full whitepaper cites five Nobel laureates and foundational papers in distributed trust, Sybil resistance, and market design.

Reputation Scoring

Logarithmic scaling follows EigenTrust (Kamvar et al., 2003 — WWW Test of Time Award). Counterparty diversity follows Douceur's Sybil cost analysis (2002). Graduated penalties follow Ostrom (Nobel 2009).

Quality Verification

Competing verifiers follow Wolfers & Zitzewitz on prediction markets (2004). Information asymmetry resolution follows Akerlof (Nobel 2001). Micropayment verification follows Coase (Nobel 1991).

Settlement Design

Escrow as commitment mechanism follows Schelling (Nobel 2005). Schema validation as contract follows Hart & Moore (1988). The hybrid oracle architecture follows Zintus-Art et al. (2025, Frontiers in Blockchain).
